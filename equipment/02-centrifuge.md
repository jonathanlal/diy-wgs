# Equipment: Microcentrifuge

**Decision status:** OPEN — leaning toward cheap commercial unit

---

## What It Does
Spins samples at high speed to pellet cells or separate layers by density.
Used at multiple points: pelleting buccal cells, Zymo extraction steps.

---

## Speed Requirements
| Step | Required Speed | Notes |
|------|---------------|-------|
| Buccal cell pelleting | 4,000 × g | Lower speed, most centrifuges can do this |
| Zymo column binding/wash/elute | 8,000 × g | This is the critical requirement |
| Brief spins / pulse | ~2,000 × g | Any centrifuge |

**Critical:** Must reach **8,000 × g minimum** for the Zymo miniprep kit.
The Bento Lab Standard (not Pro) can't reach this speed — that's why the author needed the Pro.

8,000 × g in a standard 1.5mL rotor ≈ ~10,000–12,000 RPM depending on rotor radius.

---

## Options

### 1. Cheap Commercial Microcentrifuge — RECOMMENDED
Budget benchtop microcentrifuges that reach 10,000–14,000 RPM:
- **Benchmark MyFuge Mini** — ~$250, reaches 14,500 RPM ✓
- **Labnet Prism R** — ~$300, 14,000 RPM, refrigerated option
- **Generic 1.5mL microcentrifuge on Amazon/eBay** — $150–200, verify RPM spec carefully

**What to look for:**
- Max speed ≥ 10,000 RPM
- Holds 1.5mL tubes (standard) and 0.2mL PCR tubes (some use adapter)
- Fixed-angle rotor
- Balancing: must have even number of rotor slots to counterbalance

Cost: ~$200–300

### 2. Used Lab Microcentrifuge — BEST VALUE
Used Eppendorf, Thermo Fisher, or Beckman units on eBay:
- Eppendorf 5415D/5424: routinely $100–200 used, excellent quality
- Thermo Heraeus Fresco: similar price range
- These are the same units used in real labs — reliable and well-documented

Cost: ~$100–200 used

### 3. Bento Lab Pro — NOT RECOMMENDED
- $1,800 for centrifuge + thermocycler + gel electrophoresis in one unit
- The gel electrophoresis unit is not needed for this workflow
- Paying a large premium for a bundled feature we won't use
- Author liked the aesthetics, we care about cost

### 4. DIY Centrifuge — NOT RECOMMENDED for this speed
- Centrifuge designs using 3D printed rotors, salvaged DC motors, etc. exist online
- At 4,000 × g, DIY could work
- At 8,000–14,000 × g, a rotor failure is genuinely dangerous — catastrophic failure sends shards outward at high velocity
- Not worth the risk when commercial units cost $200

**A centrifuge is one of the few items where "just buy the cheap commercial one" is the right answer.**

---

## Balancing — Critical Safety Note
Every spin must be balanced. Two approaches:
1. Always run two samples at once and counterbalance them against each other
2. When running a single sample, fill a second tube with water to the same volume and place it in the opposite rotor position

Unbalanced centrifuges vibrate destructively, can damage the rotor, and in worst cases can cause the rotor to fail.

---

## Verdict
**Buy a used Eppendorf 5415D/5424 or equivalent on eBay. ~$100–200.**
Verify it reaches 10,000+ RPM. This is a solved problem — no need to build.
Alternatively, any new budget unit ≥10,000 RPM for ~$250 works fine.
